Cypress species for plantation forestry in Northland


One of the primary reasons why lusitanica cypress is not considered viable by the forest industry here in New Zealand is that radiata pine regimes have been traditionally applied to the species, increasing costs and lowering returns.  What is required is a cost effective and appropriate silvicultural system for cypress, one that demonstrates optumum returns and reduced costs.

There are three key areas where radiata management lets cypress growers down:
i) Initial stocking;
ii) Pruning technology and thinning techniques;
iii) Rotation length, final crop stocking and target log diameters.

I have developed a set of management skills designed to be simple and foolproof and applicable to any scale of plantation.

 Establishment
The optimum regime starts with planting a high stock density. Trees must be planted at a density of at least 1700 stems per hectare.

Radiata pine costs around $1000-$1500 a hectare to prepare, plant and establish, while lusitanica costs around $2000-$2500 (at 1700 stems per hectare).
This is the only cost that is actually higher than for radiata. Pruning and thinning costs with this initial stocking may actually be lower. Research should focus on comparative studies to evaluate this.

Advantages:
  • Straight stems, fine branching;
  • high selection ratio from existing unimproved seedlines, thus ensuring retention of only the most vigorous, well formed and canker-free stems;
  • much less toppling though the initial critical stages of establishment;
  • Much lower risk of storm damage because the trees are gradually and progressively thinned, and traditional pruning/thinning regimes adopted from radiata management leave trees open and exposed. The key to success is managing lusitanica plantations for resilience;
  • Lower pruning cost because only crop trees are pruned, and branching is finer;
  • Thinning costs are reduced because thinning is undertaken at the same time as pruning, by the pruner and with the same tools;
  • High stocking and upwardly competing trees means pruning and selection can be delayed until appropriate. This is important because under a traditional wider spaced regime, the first pruning lift is "hit and miss" because it takes place prior to selection for vigour, form and absence of canker;
  • The "window of opportunity" to prune and thin is extended and management is proactive rather than reactive. There is much more room for error- "followers" are always available to be pruned if necessary, because they retain narrow form, small diameters and small branches;
  • Cost-effective pruning of up to 3m per lift can be achieved (5-6m for followers);
  • Effective weed control achieved by fast closing canopy. By the time pruning work is required, easy access is ensured, and thus lower pruning costs.

Pruning
Cypress trees have abundant small branches which are very time consuming to prune off with loppers, as used in pine tree pruning.
I have developed a pruning saw which is very fast and easy to use, while not fatiguing the operator. This modified reciprocating saw is demonstrated with this video.

Thinning
Sudden opening up of the canopy caused by excessive thinning can be very detrimental to cypress plantations, resulting in toppling of prime crop trees thus leaving gaps in the forest. This makes surviving trees more vulnerable to further storm damage. 
Thinning operations ideally should be undertaken on a regular and gradual basis, which is expensive.
Ringbarking of competing trees can be undertaken at the same time as pruning, using the same modified reciprocating saw. Only trees directly adjacent to and directly competing with the crop tree being pruned require ringbarking. During each pruning operation more trees are ringbarked, thus ensuring a gradual and more natural "thinning" of the forest. The trees remain standing and dead, and thus do not impede access like felled thinning residues do. Easy access means fast progress and significant cost savings.
Not only are costs significantly reduced but crown branch size is also minimised above pruned log height by the progressive and gradual reduction of stocking densities. This means higher value top logs.

Final crop stocking
Final crop stocking is the subject of much debate but I recommend a relatively high stocking for two reasons:

1. Cypress trees are subject to wind damage and New Zealands winter conditions of high winds and wet soils can lead to toppling of trees of all ages. The low tree stockings required for long rotations and big logs are particularly vulnerable, and vulnerability increases with each thinning operation and the long time periods to harvest. Forests thinned heavily for large diameters do not have replacements for toppled trees and inevitably suffer from large gaps and resulting reduction in yields;

2. Sawmilling technology is becoming more and more able to deal with small diameter logs. Closer spacing between trees means smaller branching in the headlogs and thus more value even from small diameters. Hewsaws deal with diameters down to 8cm and with a maximum of 25cm. The R200 Mobile HewSaw Sawmill can be used for processing small runs of logs at various locations so is suitable for a smaller more widespread resource. Industry needs to think ahead.

More research needs to take place on determining optimum log diameters for efficient production of sawn timber, thus leading to optimum rotation lengths and stocking densities. This is an important and clear research priority which will clarify the economics for industry. Results may just be surprising. There has been some historical work (see below) but this has not yet produced valid results for industry.

Small diameter cypress logs can be sawn with good yields. I would suggest that aiming for a 20 year rotation with 400-500 stems per hectare is appropriate, and will produce higher annual returns than radiata. Something for industry to think about...
